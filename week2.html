Week 2

Percentages

A percentage is a special type of fraction. The word "percent" literally means "per hundred".

This means that any percentage can be written as a fraction with denominator 100.

\[ 25\% = \frac{25}{100} \]

Percentages are very common in engineering, finance and everyday calculations. They are often used to describe increases, decreases and taxes such as VAT.

Example 1 - Finding a percentage of a value

Find 20% of 50.

\[ \begin{aligned} 20\% &= \frac{20}{100} \\ \frac{20}{100} \times 50 &= \frac{1000}{100} = 10 \end{aligned} \]

So 20% of 50 is 10.

Example 2 - Subtracting a percentage

A product costs £80. It is discounted by 25%.

First calculate 25% of 80.

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{25}{100} \times 80 &= 20 \end{aligned} \]

The discount is £20.

\[ 80 - 20 = 60 \]

The new price is £60.

Example 3 - Finding the price before VAT

A product costs £150 including VAT. The VAT rate is 20%. Find the original price before VAT.

If VAT is 20%, then the final price represents 120% of the original price.

\[ 120\% = \frac{120}{100} \] \[ \begin{aligned} \text{Original price}\times \frac{120}{100} &= 150 \end{aligned} \]

Divide both sides by \(\frac{120}{100}\), which is same as multiplying by \(\frac{100}{120}\):

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{Original price} &= 150 \times \frac{100}{120} =125\\ \end{aligned} \]

The original price before VAT was £125.

Important: Remember that the 20% VAT is calculated from the original price, not from the final price that already includes VAT.

This is a very common mistake. People sometimes try to find the original price by simply subtracting 20% from the final price, but this is incorrect!

Ratios

A ratio is a way of comparing quantities. It shows the relationship between two or more numbers.

Ratios are usually written using a colon:

\[ a : b \]

For example:

\[ 2 : 3 \]

This means that for every 2 parts of the first quantity, there are 3 parts of the second quantity.

The video below explains the basic idea of ratios and how they can be used to compare quantities and solve practical problems.

Powers (Indices)

A power (or index) tells us how many times a number is multiplied by itself.

\[ a^n \]

This means the number a is multiplied by itself n times.

Example:

\[ 5^3 = 5 \times 5 \times 5 \]

This is equal to:

\[ 5^3 = 125 \]

Multiplying powers

When multiplying powers with the same base, we add the exponents.

\[ a^n \times a^m = a^{n+m} \]

Example:

\[ 5^2 \times 5^3 \]

Write it out:

\[ 5^2 = 5 \times 5 \] \[ 5^3 = 5 \times 5 \times 5 \]

So together we have:

\[ 5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5 = 5^5 \]

Dividing powers

When dividing powers with the same base, we subtract the exponents.

\[ a^n \div a^m = a^{n-m} \]

Example:

\[ 5^5 \div 5^3 \]

Write it out:

\[ \frac{5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5}{5 \times 5 \times 5} \]

Three 5's cancel out, leaving:

\[ 5^2 \]

Power of zero

Any number raised to the power of zero equals 1.

\[ a^0 = 1 \]

Example:

\[ \frac{5^3}{5^3} \]

Using the rule \(a^n \div a^m = a^{n-m}\):

\[ \frac{5^3}{5^3} = 5^{3-3} = 5^0 \]

But we also know that any number divided by itself equals 1.

\[ \frac{5^3}{5^3} = 1 \]

Therefore:

\[ 5^0 = 1 \]

Negative powers

A negative power means the reciprocal.

\[ a^{-1} = \frac{1}{a} \] Example: \[ 5^{-1} = \frac{1}{5} \] \[ 5^{-2} = \frac{1}{5^2} = \frac{1}{25} \]

Roots as powers

Roots can also be written using powers.

Square root: \[ \sqrt{a} = a^{1/2} \] Cube root: \[ \sqrt[3]{a} = a^{1/3} \]

Power of a power

Sometimes a power is raised to another power. In this case we multiply the exponents.

\[ (a^n)^m = a^{n \times m} \]

Example:

\[ (5^2)^3 \]

First write it out:

\[ (5^2)^3 = (5 \times 5)^3 \]

This means we multiply the group three times:

\[ (5 \times 5) \times (5 \times 5) \times (5 \times 5) \]

Counting all the 5's gives:

\[ 5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5 = 5^6 \]

So:

\[ (5^2)^3 = 5^{2 \times 3} = 5^6 \] Fractional powers

A fractional exponent combines a power and a root.

Example: \[ 2^{2/3} \]

This means:

First take the cube root, then square the result.

\[ 2^{2/3} = (\sqrt[3]{2})^2 \]

Or equivalently:

\[ 2^{2/3} = \sqrt[3]{2^2} \]

Week 2 Exercises

These exercises cover the topics from Week 2: percentages, ratios and powers. The difficulty increases as you go down the list. Try to solve the problems yourself before checking the solution.

Exercise 1

Find 10% of 80.

Show solution \[ 10\% = \frac{10}{100} \] \[ \frac{10}{100} \times 80 = 8 \]

The answer is 8.

Exercise 2

Find 25% of 200.

Show solution \[ 25\% = \frac{25}{100} \] \[ \frac{25}{100} \times 200 = 50 \]

The answer is 50.

Exercise 3

A product costs £120. It is discounted by 10%.

What is the new price?

Show solution \[ 10\% \times 120 = 12 \] \[ 120 - 12 = 108 \]

The new price is £108.

Exercise 4

An item costs £60 before VAT. VAT is 20%. What is the final price including VAT?

Show solution \[ 20\% \times 60 = 12 \] \[ 60 + 12 = 72 \]

The final price is £72.

Exercise 5

An epoxy adhesive must be mixed in the ratio:

\[ 3 : 1 \]

If you use 300 g of resin, how much hardener is required?

Show solution

300 g corresponds to the 3 parts.

\[ \frac{300}{3} = 100 \]

1 part = 100 g

Hardener = 1 part

\[ 1 \times 100 = 100 \]

You need 100 g of hardener.

Exercise 6

A mixture has the ratio:

\[ 2 : 5 \]

If the first component is 40 g, how much of the second component is required?

Show solution

40 g corresponds to 2 parts.

\[ \frac{40}{2} = 20 \]

1 part = 20 g

Second component = 5 parts

\[ 5 \times 20 = 100 \]

The second component is 100 g.

Exercise 7

Simplify:

\[ 2^3 \times 2^4 \]
Show solution \[ 2^3 \times 2^4 = 2^{3+4} \] \[ = 2^7 \]

Exercise 8

Simplify:

\[ 5^6 \div 5^2 \]
Show solution \[ 5^6 \div 5^2 = 5^{6-2} \] \[ = 5^4 \]

Exercise 9

Evaluate:

\[ 4^0 \]
Show solution \[ a^0 = 1 \] \[ 4^0 = 1 \]

Exercise 10

Evaluate:

\[ 5^{-2} \]
Show solution \[ 5^{-2} = \frac{1}{5^2} \] \[ = \frac{1}{25} \]

Exercise 11

Simplify:

\[ (3^2)^4 \]
Show solution \[ (3^2)^4 = 3^{2 \times 4} \] \[ = 3^8 \]

Exercise 12

Rewrite using roots:

\[ 16^{1/2} \]
Show solution \[ 16^{1/2} = \sqrt{16} \] \[ = 4 \]

Exercise 13

Rewrite using roots:

\[ 27^{1/3} \]
Show solution \[ 27^{1/3} = \sqrt[3]{27} \] \[ = 3 \]

Exercise 14

Simplify:

\[ 3^{2/3} \]
Show solution \[ 3^{2/3} = (\sqrt[3]{3})^2 \ or \ = \sqrt[3]{3^2} \]

Take the cube root of 3 and then square the result.Or square 3 first and then take the cube root. Both give the same answer.

Exercise 15 (Challenge)

Simplify:

\[ 3^2 \times 3^4 \div 3^3 \]
Show solution \[ 3^{2+4} \div 3^3 \] \[ = 3^{6-3} \] \[ = 3^3 \] \[ = 27 \]
