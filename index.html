Week 1

Number line

The number line is a simple visual way to understand numbers and basic arithmetic. It represents numbers arranged in order along a straight line.

Zero sits in the middle. Numbers become larger as we move to the right, and smaller as we move to the left.

Fractions

A fraction represents a part of a whole.

A fraction has two nummbers:

\[ \frac{a}{b} \]
  • Numerator (top number) - how many parts we have
  • Denominator (bottom number) - how many equal parts the whole is divided into

Example:

\[ \frac{3}{4} \]

This means the whole has been divided into four equal parts, and we take three of those parts.

Another useful way to think about fractions is that they represent division.

\[ \frac{3}{4} = 3 \div 4 \]

Improper Fractions

A fraction where the numerator is larger than the denominator is called an improper fraction.

Example:

\[ \frac{5}{3} \]

This means the value is greater than 1.

Adding Fractions

To add fractions, we need to have a common denominator. This means we need to express both fractions with the same bottom number.

Example:

\[ \frac{1}{4} + \frac{2}{4}= \frac{3}{4} \]

If the denominators are different, they must first be made the same.

Example:

\[ \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} \]

Convert to the same denominator:

\[ \frac{1}{2}=\frac{1 \times 2}{2 \times 2} = \frac{2}{4} \]

Then add:

\[ \frac{2}{4} + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{3}{4} \]

In mathematics, it is considered more elegant to use the least common denominator when working with fractions. It makes the fractions easier to read and allows calculations to be carried out with smaller numbers.

However, it is important to remember that using the least common denominator isnot strictly necessary to obtain the correct result. Other common denominators will also work, although they may lead to larger numbers and less tidy calculations.
